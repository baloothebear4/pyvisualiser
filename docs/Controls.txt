You’re exactly right: once GPU performance is solved, visual quality becomes an artistic systems problem, not a coding
one. The best visualisers succeed because they control visual hierarchy — what the eye sees first, second, and third —
rather than simply drawing interesting shapes.
Your instinct to start with backgrounds and outlines is perfect because these form the perceptual frame that everything
else sits inside.

Let’s break this down into principles used in professional motion graphics and visualisation design.

1. Backgrounds: The Silent Composer
The background sets mood, depth, and contrast but should never compete with the data.
Think of it as the stage lighting for the orchestra.

Golden Rule
Background contrast should be 70–90% darker than the brightest foreground element.
If backgrounds get too bright:

glow effects look muddy
shapes lose definition
the visualiser feels chaotic
The Three Background Types
These cover almost all successful visualisers.
A. Pure Black / Near Black
Used by many classic visualisers.
Examples:

Winamp Milkdrop
Resolume Arena VJ scenes
Advantages:
maximum contrast
glow looks beautiful
extremely clear
Typical colour:
RGB: 0–10
But pure black can feel sterile unless balanced with glow.
B. Gradient Atmosphere
A subtle gradient gives depth without distraction.
Example gradient:

Top: RGB(5,5,10)
Bottom: RGB(0,0,0)
Benefits:
adds subtle cinematic depth
helps central elements pop
avoids “void” feeling
C. Procedural / Audio Reactive Background
Used carefully, this is very powerful.
Examples:

subtle noise
blurred particles
extremely slow colour drift
But keep amplitude very low.
Rule:

Background motion < 10% intensity of foreground motion Otherwise the eye cannot settle. 2. Outlines: The Secret Weapon
  Outlines are massively underrated in visualisers. They provide edge definition, which the human eye relies on. Without
  outlines, glow effects often become mush. There are three main types. A. Hard Vector Outline A thin solid line around
  shapes. Example: line_width=1–2 px alpha=0.5–0.8 Benefits: improves clarity stabilises shapes prevents blur collapse
  Often white or slightly tinted. B. Additive Glow Outline This is a blurred halo around shapes. Implementation usually
  involves: shape → blur → add blend This creates energy and movement. But the trick: glow should never exceed the
  brightness of the core shape. Otherwise shapes lose definition. C. Dual Outline (Most Beautiful) Used in many high-end
  visualisers. Structure: core shape thin bright outline soft outer glow Example stack: shape fill outline (1px bright)
  glow (6–12px blur) This creates crisp + ethereal simultaneously. 3. Depth Layers (Extremely Important) Natural visuals
  usually have 3 depth planes. Background Low contrast Low motion Midground Most visualisers live here. Foreground High
  glow / highlight elements Example: particles (background) bars or waves (midground) peak highlights (foreground) This
  layering stops visuals feeling flat. 4. Colour Strategy A common mistake is using too many colours. Great visualisers
  often limit to: 1 primary colour 1 accent colour white highlights Example palette: Deep blue cyan accent white peaks
  This creates visual coherence. 5. Negative Space (Often Overlooked) Beautiful visuals always leave space for the eye
  to rest. Rules of thumb: 30–50% of screen empty If the entire screen moves constantly it becomes tiring. 6. Motion
  Smoothness Even with good shapes, motion determines beauty. Key techniques: Easing Avoid direct amplitude mapping. Use
  smoothing: smoothed=lerp(prev, new, 0.2) Low Frequency Drift Slow movement adds life. Example: rotation +=0.01 * time
  7. Blur & Glow Physics (Important for Your Project) Glow should behave like light scattering. Good glow settings: blur
  radius: 6–20 px intensity: 0.2–0.5 blend: additive Avoid huge blur radii because they flatten the image. 8. Visual
  Hierarchy (Critical) Every frame should answer: 1️⃣ What is the main feature? 2️⃣ What supports it? 3️⃣ What stays
  quiet? Example: Main: bass visualiser Support: treble sparks Quiet: background gradient Without hierarchy visuals feel
  noisy. 9. One Trick Used in the Best Visualisers Many top visualisers subtly darken the centre or edges using a
  vignette. It focuses attention. Example shader idea: brightness *=smoothstep(1.2, 0.3, distance_from_center) Very
  subtle but powerful. 10. A Design Rule That Might Help PyVisualiser Think of each visualiser as a visual instrument.
